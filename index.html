🧭 1. The Philosophy of Home Education
🎯 Your Goal
You are not trying to replicate school at home. You are building a thinker, a reader, and a capable human being. School teaches to the middle. You can teach to this specific child. That is your superpower.

Aim for: depth over coverage, mastery over speed, curiosity over compliance.
📐 The 80/20 of Education
80% of academic success comes from:
  • Reading fluency — a child who reads well learns everything faster
  • Mathematical fluency — number sense unlocks all STEM
  • Writing clearly — organises thought and communicates ideas
Everything else builds on these three. Protect them. Do them daily. Never skip them.
🏠 Life IS the Classroom
Cooking = maths + chemistry + life skills.
Shopping = budgeting + maths + social skills.
A walk = geography + biology + PE.
A family discussion = philosophy + English + social-emotional learning.

Homeschool has a secret weapon: context. Use it every day.
⏱ How Long Each Day?
  • Foundation (3–5): 1–1.5 hrs of structured play-learning
  • Grades 1–3: 2.5–3 hrs focused work
  • Grades 4–6: 3–4 hrs focused work
  • Grades 7–9: 4–5 hrs focused work

Homeschool is more efficient than school — no wasted transitions, no waiting for 30 others. Quality beats quantity.

🧪 3. The 4-Step Lesson Method (Use for EVERY lesson)
1
Teach & Explain (15 min) — Introduce the concept using a real-life example first. Never start abstract. Example: before teaching fractions, cut an apple in half. Ask questions: "How many pieces? What do we call each piece?"
2
Guided Practice (20 min) — Work through 3–5 examples together. Think aloud. Show your working. Let the child complete the final step each time. Correct gently and immediately.
3
Independent Check (10 min) — Give exactly 5 questions. Child works alone. You mark immediately. Below 3/5 = reteach now. 3/5 = review tomorrow. 5/5 = move forward.
4
Real-Life Connection (5 min) — Ask: "Where will you see this in real life?" Then show it. Maths → count change. Writing → label objects in the house. Science → observe it outside. This is the glue that makes learning stick.
✅ 4. What Works vs What Doesn't
✅ DO These Things
  • Start with maths every single morning
  • Read aloud together — even to older children
  • Ask "why?" and "how do you know?" constantly
  • Celebrate effort, not just results
  • Use mastery checkpoints before moving on
  • Connect every lesson to real life
  • Allow the child to teach YOU what they learned
  • Keep a simple weekly progress journal
  • Give brain breaks — physical movement matters
  • Review previous concepts weekly (spaced repetition)
  • Let the child's curiosity lead bonus learning
  • Take a full day off when energy is depleted
❌ NEVER Do These
  • Rush through content to "finish the curriculum"
  • Move on before a topic is mastered
  • Allow screens during learning time
  • Teach for more than 90 mins without a break
  • Compare this child to any other child
  • Lose your temper during a lesson — stop, reset
  • Skip maths or English because "we'll catch up"
  • Ignore your own stress — it transfers to the child
  • Give passive homework (copy this 10 times)
  • Let days go by without reading happening
  • Label the child as "bad at" any subject permanently
📊 5. How to Assess Without Tests
🗣 Verbal Assessment
Ask the child to explain a concept back to you in their own words. If they can teach it, they know it. Prompt: "Pretend I've never heard of fractions — explain it to me."
📝 5-Question Daily Check
Every lesson ends with 5 independent questions. Score:
5/5 = mastered, move on
3–4/5 = review tomorrow
0–2/5 = reteach using a different approach today
📁 Portfolio Method
Keep a folder of the child's best work each month. Review together. Ask: "Compare your writing now to last month. What's improved?" This builds self-awareness and pride.
🔁 Spaced Repetition Review
Every Friday: revisit one concept from earlier in the week, one from last week, one from last month. This cements long-term memory far better than any single test. Use the mastery checklists as your review guide.
📆 Monthly Review Session
One session per month dedicated entirely to review — no new content. Cover the mastery checkpoints for the month across all subjects. Mark what's solid (✓), what needs work (△), and what to reteach (✗).
🏆 Project-Based Assessment
Quarterly projects integrate multiple subjects. A child who plans a pretend trip to another country is using maths (budget), geography (maps), English (write an itinerary), science (climate), and history (culture). Assess the process, not just the output.
📖 6. Subject-Specific Teaching Tips
∑ Mathematics
  • Times tables by age 9 — drill daily, games help
  • Never skip mental maths — 10 mins every morning
  • Use manipulatives (beans, coins, sticks) for early concepts
  • Word problems first, not last
  • If they can't explain their method, they don't understand it
  • Red flag: Cannot recall number bonds to 10 by Grade 2 → pause and fix this before anything else
✒ English & Reading
  • Phonics before sight words — always
  • Read aloud daily — 20 minutes minimum, any age
  • Writing stamina builds slowly — start with sentences, not essays
  • Grammar through reading, not drills
  • Vocabulary: 5 new words per week, used in sentences
  • Red flag: Cannot read simple sentences by end of Grade 1 → intensive phonics intervention needed immediately
⚗ Science
  • Always connect to Kenya and East Africa — local context first
  • Simple experiments weekly — even if messy
  • Science journals: draw and label observations
  • Ask hypothesis questions: "What do you think will happen?"
  • Nature walks count as science
🌍 Languages (French/Arabic/Spanish)
  • Short daily sessions (20–30 min) far outperform weekly marathons
  • Songs and rhymes are the fastest path for young children
  • Label household objects in the target language
  • Aim for: greetings → colours → numbers → family → body → food → school
  • Do not stress about grammar until reading in English is solid (Grade 3+)
  • Native media (cartoons, music) supplements learning powerfully
🏠 Life Skills
  • Teach by doing — cook together, not just discuss it
  • Give real responsibility: pocket money, a small household job
  • The goal is a capable adult, not a compliant child
  • Include money literacy every week — it's never too early
  • Let them fail at small things safely so they learn to recover
🎨 Creative Arts & PE
  • Art is not optional — creativity develops problem-solving
  • 30 min of physical activity every day — non-negotiable
  • Music: daily singing is enough for early years
  • Don't grade creative work — celebrate it
  • Let the child choose topics for free art sessions
🚨 7. Warning Signs & When to Adjust
Stop and address these immediately — do not push forward
  • Cannot read CVC words by end of Grade 1 — intensive phonics programme needed
  • Does not know all times tables by end of Grade 4 — daily drill until mastered before any algebra
  • Consistent resistance / tearfulness about a specific subject — may indicate learning difficulty; assess and adjust approach
  • Copying without understanding — slow down, return to concrete manipulatives
  • You are teaching the same concept for a 3rd time — change the method entirely, not just repeat
  • Tutor exhaustion or frustration — take a day off. One bad day of teaching does lasting harm. Rest is part of the plan.
✅ Signs you're doing it right: The child asks questions you haven't taught yet. They connect lessons to real life spontaneously. They want to read for pleasure. They can explain their maths working. They say "I don't know yet" instead of "I can't." These are the signals of a thriving home learner.
📦 8. Recommended Resources (No-cost & Low-cost)
📱 Free Digital Tools
  • Khan Academy — complete Maths K-12, free
  • BBC Bitesize — excellent Science & English support
  • Duolingo — French, Spanish, Arabic supplementary language practice
  • YouTube: TED-Ed — short explainer videos for any topic
  • LibriVox — free audiobooks for read-aloud time
  • Google Earth — geography made real
📚 Essential Physical Materials
  • Exercise books (maths squared, lined for writing)
  • A globe or physical world map on the wall
  • A ruler, protractor, compass set (from Grade 4)
  • Multiplication table poster in the learning space
  • A dictionary and thesaurus (physical, Grade 3+)
  • Flashcards (DIY works fine)
  • A simple science kit: magnifying glass, measuring tape, scales
🗂 Organising Your Records
  • Weekly log: subjects covered, time spent, observations
  • Monthly mastery review: tick off checkpoints
  • Portfolio folder: best piece of work per month, per subject
  • Annual assessment: use a past Cambridge Primary paper to benchmark (Grades 4–6)
  • Keep a "great questions the child asked" journal — it reveals their thinking
